# Lincoln-Douglas (LD) Debate Guide

## Overview
One-on-one. Aff defends the resolution; Neg opposes. Named for the Lincoln-Douglas 1858 debates. Resolutions focus on **philosophical, ethical, and value-based questions** — not specific government policies. Asks which values or principles should take precedence when they conflict. Topics change **every two months** (NSDA level). Debaters must argue both sides.

Example resolutions: "Resolved: A just government ought to prioritize the rule of law over jury nullification." / "Resolved: Rehabilitation ought to be valued above retribution in the criminal justice system."

## Speech Order & Times (6-3-7-3-4-6-3)

| Speech | Time | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|
| 1AC | 6 min | Pre-scripted Aff case |
| Neg Cross-Examination | 3 min | Neg questions Aff |
| 1NC | 7 min | Neg case (attacks Aff AND establishes Neg position) |
| Aff Cross-Examination | 3 min | Aff questions Neg |
| 1AR | 4 min | **Hardest speech** — must answer all 7 min of 1NC AND rebuild Aff case |
| NR (2NR) | 6 min | Neg's final speech; collapse to 1–2 positions |
| 2AR | 3 min | Aff's final speech; crystallize the round |

**Prep time:** ~4 minutes per debater total, used between speeches.

**Drop rule:** Any argument not answered in the 1AR is considered conceded and cannot be revived in the 2AR.
**No new arguments in 2AR.**

## Framework — The Most Distinctive Feature of LD

Every case opens with a **Value Premise** and a **Value Criterion**.

### Value Premise
The abstract ultimate ethical goal the debater argues their side achieves. It's the overarching principle the round is about.

Common values: Justice, Liberty, Morality, Human Dignity, Societal Welfare, Autonomy, Equality, Democracy, Security, Life

### Value Criterion (Standard)
A more specific mechanism or standard that defines how to achieve or measure the value.

Example: Value = Morality → Criterion = Utilitarianism ("utilitarianism is the most defensible framework for evaluating moral outcomes")

**Why framework matters:** The framework sets the philosophical lens for the entire round. If the judge accepts your value and criterion, your contentions are more likely to count. Winning the framework debate is often decisive.

## Contentions
Concrete arguments tied back to the framework.
- **Claim** — the argument being made
- **Warrant** — evidence or philosophical reasoning that supports it
- **Impact** — why this argument matters and how it achieves the value
- Typically 2–3 contentions per case. All must link back to the framework.

## Neg Strategy Options
1. **Direct refutation** — attack Aff's evidence, reasoning, or specific examples
2. **Counter-framework** — propose different value or criterion that leads to a Neg conclusion
3. **Turns** — show Aff's arguments actually support Neg
4. **Concede value, win criterion** — agree on the value but argue your criterion better achieves it
5. **Burden argument** — Neg only needs to show Aff failed their burden, not prove the opposite

## Evidence vs. Philosophy
Unlike policy debate, **philosophical analysis, logical syllogisms, and ethical theory** are as important as quoted evidence. Kant, Mill, Rawls, Nozick, Locke are common sources.

## Traditional vs. National Circuit LD

| | Traditional / Local | National Circuit |
|--|--|--|
| Speed | Conversational | Spreading (fast delivery) |
| Evidence | Minimal | Heavy card use |
| Theory | Rare | Common (borrowed from policy) |
| Frameworks | Value/criterion focused | Complex philosophical + theory |
| Judges | Community members, coaches | Experienced circuit judges |

## Organizations
- **HS**: NSDA (primary), NCFL (National Catholic Forensic League), Stoa USA. TOC requires qualifying bids.
- **College equivalent**: NFA-LD (National Forensic Association LD) — uses policy-topic resolution for full season, one-on-one format with evidence-based policy arguments, more conversational style than NDT/CEDA, lower barrier to entry
- Some college LD debaters transition to NDT/CEDA policy or APDA parliamentary debate (impromptu, no pre-researched evidence, value/policy topics)

## Common Critical Arguments in National Circuit LD
- **Theory**: procedural fairness arguments (AFC bad, NIBs, disclosure theory)
- **Kritiks**: similar to policy — cap, security, identity
- **Frivolous theory**: targeted theory arguments against unusual 1AC moves
- **Pre-fiat vs. post-fiat distinction**: whether the K operates at the level of in-round performance or real-world policy

## Tips
- In the 1AR, prioritize and collapse — you cannot go for everything
- The NR (2NR) should collapse to the single best path to victory
- Framework debates in traditional LD are often decided by which debater more clearly explains why their criterion achieves the shared value
- On the national circuit, disclosure on Wikispaces (open disclosure) is expected — cases should be disclosed before rounds
