# Public Forum (PF) Debate Guide

## Overview
Two-on-two. Pro defends the resolution; Con opposes. Explicitly designed for **lay judges** — community members, parents, educators without debate backgrounds. Arguments must be clear, jargon-free, and persuasive to a general audience. No spreading. No heavy theory. No philosophical Ks. Topics focus on current events: foreign policy, economics, domestic policy, ethics, global affairs. Topics change **monthly** — teams prep both sides each month.

## Coin Flip
Before each round, the winner chooses EITHER (a) which side to debate (Pro/Con) OR (b) whether to speak first or second. The loser gets the remaining choice.

## Speech Order

| Speech | Time | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|
| Pro Constructive | 4 min | Pre-written case, 2–3 contentions |
| Con Constructive | 4 min | Pre-written case, 2–3 contentions |
| Crossfire 1 | 3 min | 1st speakers from each team |
| Pro Rebuttal | 4 min | 2nd speaker attacks Con's case |
| Con Rebuttal | 4 min | 2nd speaker attacks Pro's case |
| Crossfire 2 | 3 min | 2nd speakers from each team |
| Pro Summary | 3 min | 1st speaker consolidates; sets up final push |
| Con Summary | 3 min | Mirror of Pro Summary |
| Grand Crossfire | 3 min | All 4 debaters |
| Pro Final Focus | 2 min | Last Pro speech; crystallize 1–2 most important arguments |
| Con Final Focus | 2 min | Last speech of the round |

**Prep time:** 2–3 minutes shared per team, used whenever between speeches.

**No new arguments in Summary or Final Focus** — these speeches crystallize and extend arguments already in the round.

## Argument Structure
- **Contention** = Claim + Warrant (evidence or reasoning) + Impact (why it matters)
- Typically 2–3 contentions per constructive

## Weighing
Compare impacts to decide who wins the round:
- **Magnitude** — how large is the harm or benefit?
- **Probability** — how likely is it to occur?
- **Timeframe** — how soon does it happen?
- Winning the weighing debate often decides the round. Run your weighing framework in Summary and Final Focus.

## Evidence Standards
- Must come from credible sources: news orgs, academic journals, think tanks, government reports
- **Misrepresenting evidence is strictly prohibited**
- Paraphrasing is allowed but the original source must be available if challenged
- Unlike policy, PF does not use the card-cutting format — debaters paraphrase or read short direct quotes

## Crossfire
Debaters question each other directly — conversational and simultaneous (both can ask AND answer). Not one-sided like policy CX.
- **Goals**: expose logical flaws, secure admissions for use later in round, poke holes
- **Grand Crossfire** (all 4 debaters): often the most strategic part of the round; use it to control the narrative going into Summary

## Judging
Lay judges are the norm, especially at local tournaments. National circuit has shifted toward faster delivery and more technical structures (some theory), but the format officially discourages this.
- Prioritize: clarity, clash, real-world impacts, persuasion
- Avoid jargon when the judge is a lay judge
- Speaker points awarded alongside W/L

## Organizations
- **HS**: NSDA (primary), TOC (Tournament of Champions — requires qualifying bids)
- **No direct college equivalent** — college students transition to NPDA or APDA (parliamentary formats, impromptu prep, 15–20 min before round, no pre-researched evidence files, weekly tournaments at member universities)

## Common Mistakes
- Running new arguments in Summary/Final Focus (illegal)
- Not extending key arguments through Summary into Final Focus (they die if dropped)
- Ignoring weighing until Final Focus (too late)
- Speaking too fast for lay judges
- Not flowing crossfire — admissions from crossfire must be used in speeches
